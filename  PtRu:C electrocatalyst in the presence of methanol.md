B S T R A C T
Dissolution of PtRu alloy nanoparticles on carbon support in acidic environment is qualitatively and
quantitatively investigated using an advanced electrochemical technique: electrochemical flow cell (EFC)
on-line coupled to inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometer (ICP-MS). Profound insights into
potential-resolved corrosion stability of Ru-Pt alloy in the potential window from 0.05 to 1.6 V vs RHE are
obtained. Compared to pure Pt the PtRu alloy is less stable due to destabilisation of Pt surface caused by
Ru dissolution together with the presence of weaker Ru-Pt bonds. However, at high potentials (1.5 and
1.6 V) Pt gets stabilised, presumably via a Ru catalysed oxygen evolution reaction that acts as a cathodic
protection. Both Ru and Pt dissolution are enhanced in the presence of methanol due to consumption of
surface ruthenium oxides, presumably Ru O ,
2 3
by the methanol oxidation reaction and an inhibited
redeposition of Pt ions due to surface blockage of CO. The results may be used as useful guidelines in
designing future DMFC and PEMFC catalysts.

1. Introduction
Increasing energy consumption trends are becoming one of the
world’s major challenges, especially because they mostly rely on
increased usage of unrenewable, ecologically harmful fossil fuels.
The urge to adequately and eventually replace the conventional
energy infrastructure with sustainable solutions is accelerating the
development of electrochemical conversion and storage devices.
Batteries, electrolysers and fuel cells are expected to play a key role
in future attempts to commercialise novel sustainable solutions
[1]. As regards proton exchange membrane fuel cells (PEMFC),
their initial breakthrough on the automotive market is estimated
to occur at latest by 2020 [2,3]. However, to achieve a higher cost-
effectiveness ratio and a better commercial acceptability in
automotive and stationary applications, the corrosion stability of
the PEMFC catalyst needs to be further improved [4]. Similar
stability challenges along with activity improvements lie
direct
upon
methanol fuel cells (DMFC) which otherwise have attractive
properties suitable for portable devices and remote power
generations [5].
The state-of-the-art anode materials for methanol oxidation in
real DMFC applications remain Pt(cid:1)Ru bimetallic catalysts
supported on carbon [6]. Non-alloyed analogues are surpassing
the alloyed composites by orders of magnitude due to the presence
of Ru in hydrous oxides form [7–10]. In contrast to PEM-FC where
strategies [11–16] and procedures [17–19] for highly active oxygen
reduction (ORR) catalyst have been established, DMFC anodic
catalysts still suffer from low electrocatalytic activities [5,20,21].
Although some multi-metallic systems show better initial activi-
ties [22], in terms of commercial viability the long-term
performance is of particular importance [5,20]. In general, Pt–Ru
catalysts are stable at potentials lower than 0.5 V vs. RHE [21,23].
Hence, one might expect a very low degradation of these catalysts
since, thermodynamically, MOR occurs close to 0 V [24]. On the
other hand, practical studies show a considerable Ru dissolution in
DMFC membrane electrode assembly (MEA) during degradation
tests [25]. Thus one might reasonably presume that potential
spikes above 0.5 V are occurring. The dissolved Ru compromises
the catalytic efficiency of anode and cathode catalysts [6].

Bearing all of the above in mind, one realises that a

development of DMFC and PEMFC. The present work provides a
unique in-situ insight into metal dissolution of an industrial

flow cell (EFC) coupled to inductively coupled plasma mass
spectrometer (ICP-MS). The advantages of this approach have
already been partly demonstrated in our previous works related to
corrosion of selected electrocatalysts [35–37].

3. Results and discussion
3.1. Dissolution of ruthenium from PtRu nanoalloy
Highly sensitive ICP-MS analytics were used for online in-situ
measurements of dissolved metals during slow potentiodynamic
experiments carried out in a wide potential window (0.05–1.6 V).
This way, the operational regime potentials as well as occurrence of
high potentials as a result of reaction induced oscillations [38],
start/shutdown, short-circuit events, fuel starvation, or occur-
rences of cell reversal during long-term DMFC operation events are
usually mimicked [39,40]. Two samples were investigated: a
commercial industrial benchmark catalysts Pt/C and a PtRu/C alloy.
Both analogues were initially electrochemically activated in order
to clean the nanoparticles’ surface and to obtain a stable cyclic
voltammogram (CV). During the activation some Ru (18.7%), and
substantially less Pt (0.05%), dissolves in the Ru based analogue
whereas for the non-Ru analogue 0.45% of Pt dissolves during the
activation procedure (see, for example, Fig. 2 a in continuation).
Consequently, the Ru:Pt molar ratio changes from the initial 1:1 to
1:1.25 before carrying out the slow potentiodynamic experiment.
We note here that some surface segregation of Ru is detected
before the electrochemical experiment (see Supplementary
information, Fig. S3). Therefore the initial surface composition of
PtRu sample is rich in Ru, however no precise value could be
obtained using the present setup. By considering the surface
density of Pt atoms [41] and the electrochemically active platinum
surface area (ESA) as determined from the hydrogen under-
potential deposition (HUPD) charge after activation cycles, the
percentage of Pt monolayer was determined [35,41]. We empha-
size here that HUPD charge (such as displayed later on in Fig. 6a)
and not the more common CO stripping has been used in this
particular investigation to calculate ESA (although for comparison
we also added data for CO stripping, see Supplementary
information, Fig.S4). In the case of Ru analogue 7.4% of Pt
monolayer gets dissolved during the activation cycles. The
corresponding amount for the non-Ru analogue is even smaller
(cid:1) 1.5%. This indicates that Pt degradation was not too extensive.
Furthermore, upon slow cycling dissolution stays below approxi-
mately 2%. Since this is a relatively small change in nanoparticles
composition, the evaluation of the dissolution trends of the two
analogues during continuous slow potentiodynamic cycling is
adequate. For precise dissolution amounts representative for each
upper potential limit, fresh catalysts should be used for each
individual measurement.

originating from air exposure and especially from pre-treatment
cycles. Reduction of oxides causes disturbance of the crystal

3.2. Dissolution of platinum from PtRu nano-alloy and from Pt
particles
For PtRu nano-alloy, a well discernible Pt dissolution peak is
detected already when cycling till 0.9 V (compare Fig. 5a–c), in
contrast to our previous work [35,36]. Unlike in the presence of Ru,
in pure Pt the dissolution is noticed only above 1 V. Additionally,
the integration of dissolution profiles for consecutive potential
cycles up to 1.4 V shows consistently a higher percentage of
dissolved Pt for the PtRu catalyst (Fig. 5d). A more detailed
investigation of the Pt dissolution profiles indicates that the anodic
dissolution (Fig. 5e) of PtRu is significantly faster compared to the
pure Pt sample, whereas the difference in cathodic dissolution
(Fig. 5f) is less extensive.
Based on comparison of various recent results obtained using
highly sensitive coupled analytics, Pt dissolution has been
tentatively explained by a transient process of oxide formation/
reduction that roughens the Pt surface via formation of low-
coordinated surface atoms (or species) which are prone to
dissolution [35,41,46,47]. Interestingly, in the PtRu sample the
onset of Pt dissolution coincides with the appearance of Ru
transient dissolution (peak 2 in Fig. 1). This could indicate that
oxidation of ruthenium leads to a superficial disturbance of the
crystal structure, hence indirectly inducing release of Pt atoms. As
shown in previous theoretical [48] and experimental [49,50]
treatments of the PtRu system, Pt tends to segregate to the outer
layer. The results of the present study, however, highlight the
importance of local destabilisation of the surface/subsurface
structure due to corrosion of Ru atoms which, in turn, promotes
corrosion of Pt as well. Apart from that one must take into account
that the Pt-Ru crystal bond is weaker than the Pt-Pt bond, which
probably additionally accelerates Pt dissolution in the present case
[51].
However, when cycling till high potential regions (1.5 and
1.6 V), surprisingly, the difference in Pt cathodic corrosion
between the two analogues is diminishing (Fig. 5c,f). An inhibited
Pt corrosion has recently been shown to occur by increasing the
particle size and has been attributed to a less disruptive reduction
of surface/subsurface platinum oxide for larger particles [35].
Presumably, this is due to the fact that the average Pt atom has a
higher coordination number in larger particles than in smaller
ones (cid:1) hence the former get less disrupted by the
reduction
oxidation/
of surface oxide. However, as the particle size
distribution for the present analogues is similar (Fig. 3) this
effect should be disregarded. Instead, we propose the following
alternative explanation: (i) First we confirm that less Pt oxide is
formed in the case of PtRu, as seen from the PtO reduction
features in corresponding CVs (Fig. 6a). (ii) An obvious factor
leading to smaller amounts of formed Pt oxide is the smaller
surface area of Pt on PtRu alloy (cid:1) as the total surface area is shared
with Ru atoms. (iii) Additionally, Ru is a very good oxygen
evolution (OER) catalyst with the onset potential of approxi-
mately 1.4 V RHE (see Fig. 6a) [37,52]. Enhancement of OER in this
potential region could serve as a cathodic protection due to water
consumption and subsequent pH change at the Ru-rich regions
which, in turn, decreases the extent of oxidation processes in
neighbouring Pt-rich surface domains. (iv) Furthermore, a
detailed investigation of dissolution profiles reveals a shift in
cathodic Pt dissolution towards lower potentials which has
recently been reported for Pt-Au systems and correlated with
increased Pt stability [53,54]. Consequently less Pt dissolves at
high potentials. Implementation of Ru to cathode PEM-FC catalyst
could then be an efficient way to boost its stability during high
potential excursions.
3.3. Dissolution of ruthenium from PtRu nano-alloy in the presence of
MeOH
To the best of our knowledge, this is the first study in which
on-line dissolution monitoring was used in the presence of
methanol (0.1 M MeOH). We performed the same set of
experiments as shown and discussed in the previous sections.
Compared to non-MeOH environment, enhanced Ru dissolution
from PtRu catalyst was generally observed (Fig. 7). In fact, a
more detailed comparison shows that dissolution due to
reduction of Ru O to 2 3 Ru (peak 4c) is somewhat suppressed
whereas all the other dissolution events are more pronounced.
Taking into account this particular correlation, we propose that
MeOH is consuming Ru O through 2 3 MOR (see voltammogram in
Fig. 6b). Namely, according to the widely accepted bi-functional
Langmuir–Hinshelwood type of reaction mechanism, MeOH first
oxidises to CO on Pt sites. Then the neighbouring Ru-based site
supply oxygen to proceed the oxidation to CO [55]. 2 The present
results imply that these Ru-based active sites could be Ru O . 2 3
However, since most of the Ru O is 2 3 consumed, the correspond-
ing Ru dissolution feature is less pronounced (Fig. 7, peak 4c).
We note that the percentage of dissolved Ru during the
potentiodynamic experiment is quite low (Fig. 7d), therefore
the surface composition is presumably not significantly altered.
This is in line with only minor changes in current during the
potentiodynamic experiment in the MOR peak region and
practically no change in the MOR onset potential region
(Fig. 6b). MOR is highly sensitive to alterations of surface
composition for the PtRu catalysts [6,55]. However, it should be
stressed that due to substantially faster dissolution of Ru in
comparison to Pt (Fig. 1b–d) the Ru:Pt surface ratio is expected
to dynamically change towards Pt rich surface especially in the
case of long-term experiment. The extent of this change and the
impact on catalytic performance in a real DMFC configuration is
difficult to assess with our current experimental setup due to the
non-stagnant electrolyte regime. The redeposition of the two
metals in DMFC could therefore have influence on their surface
ratio, and hence on its catalytic activity towards MOR
[6,21,56–59].

4. Conclusions
Using a highly sensitive setup consisting of an electrochemical
flow cell coupled to ICP-MS, a detailed investigation of corrosion
mechanism of a PtRu nanoalloy catalyst was carried out. A typical
acidic environment (0.1 mol/L HClO ) 4 in the presence and absence
of methanol was used. A wide potential range (0.05 to 1.6 V vs RHE)
was investigated to cover various situations of practical interest.
The findings reveal a destabilising effect of Ru on Pt corrosion – if
compared to Pt – and are summarised as follows:
(cid:4) The increased Pt dissolution in PtRu alloy is due to destabilisa-
tion of superficial Pt caused by dissolution of Ru and,
additionally, due to weaker Ru-Pt bonds.
(cid:4) Interestingly, however, during excursions to high potentials (e.g.
to 1.5 or 1.6 V, typically found in start/stop PEM-FC events) Pt is
stabilised – presumably via a Ru catalysed oxygen evolution
reaction that acts as a cathodic protection.
(cid:4) In the presence of methanol, the dissolution of ruthenium and
platinum is enhanced. The former is due to the methanol
oxidation reaction which consumes surface oxides – presumably
Ru O . 2 3 This exposes additional ruthenium atoms. The increased
platinum dissolution is due to the inhibited re-deposition and
higher surface destabilisation caused by increased Ru dissolu-
tion.
(cid:4) The present results could be of considerable help when
designing DMFC and PEMFC catalysts.
